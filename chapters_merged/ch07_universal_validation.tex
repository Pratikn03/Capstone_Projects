\section{Universal ORIUS: Six-Domain Validation}
\label{ch:battery-to-orius}

This chapter presents the ORIUS universal validation: the DC3S shield is
proven to eliminate true-state safety violations across six distinct
cyber-physical domains---energy management, autonomous vehicles, industrial
process control, medical monitoring, aerospace, and navigation---each
evaluated at equivalent empirical depth.  Every forecasting-capable domain
carries locked telemetry, a trained forecasting surface, conformal
calibration artifacts, and a software-in-loop replay trace.  All
forecasting-capable domains show DC3S reducing true-state violations by
70\% or more under a unified fault schedule (dropout~15\%, spike~8\%,
stale~10\%).  Navigation is included as a simulation-backed portability
surface confirming that the adapter contract extends to guidance domains.

\subsection{Why the ORIUS Result Is Structurally General}

The DC3S pipeline never inspects the internal dynamics of the battery
plant.  It consumes five objects:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item An observation vector $z_t$ (telemetry readings at time $t$).
  \item A scalar reliability score $w_t \in [w_{\min}, 1]$ summarising
    telemetry quality.
  \item A conformal prediction region $C_t^{\text{RAC}}$ over the true state,
    inflated by $1/w_t$.
  \item A feasible action set $\mathcal{A}_t$ defined by domain constraints.
  \item A safe-action projection $a_t^{\text{safe}}$ that maps any candidate
    action into $\mathcal{A}_t$ under the worst-case realisation of
    $C_t^{\text{RAC}}$.
\end{enumerate}

None of these objects mention SOC, voltage, or Coulomb counting.  They are
\emph{domain-general}: any CPS providing equivalent definitions can reuse
the ORIUS pipeline subject to domain-specific validation and bounded
claim discipline.

\subsection{What Changes by Domain}

Porting ORIUS to a new domain requires replacing four domain-specific
components:

\begin{description}
  \item[Plant dynamics.] The battery uses a linear SOC integrator;
    a robotic arm requires nonlinear rigid-body dynamics.  The pipeline
    is agnostic---it consumes the predicted state set, not the equations.
  \item[State definition.] SOC is a scalar in $[0,1]$.  An autonomous
    vehicle has a six-degree-of-freedom pose.  The conformal region
    $C_t^{\text{RAC}}$ generalises to a set in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  \item[Constraint structure.] Battery constraints are box constraints
    on SOC and power.  Other domains may have polytopic, conic, or
    nonlinear constraints.  The safe-action filter requires only that
    projection onto the feasible set is computationally tractable.
  \item[Fault taxonomy.] Dropout, delay, drift, and stale-sensor faults
    are battery-specific.  Each domain has its own fault families---GPS
    multipath for vehicles, encoder drift for robots---but the OQE
    scoring architecture applies unchanged.
\end{description}

\subsection{What Remains Invariant Across Domains}

Four structural invariants transfer without modification:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{OQE architecture.}  The feature vector changes; the
    weighted scoring function and calibration procedure do not.
  \item \textbf{RAC-Cert inflation.}  The conformal prediction interval
    is domain-agnostic.  Reliability-adaptive width scaling
    $\hat{q}_\alpha / w_t$ applies to any residual distribution.
  \item \textbf{Repair projection.}  The safe-action filter projects
    onto the feasible set under worst-case uncertainty.  The projection
    operator changes with the constraint geometry; the principle of
    worst-case projection does not.
  \item \textbf{Certification logic.}  The certificate records
    $(w_t, C_t^{\text{RAC}}, a_t^{\text{safe}}, \text{margin})$
    regardless of what the symbols denote physically.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{How to Port ORIUS to a New Domain}

A practitioner deploys ORIUS on a new plant by implementing a
\texttt{DomainAdapter}---the analogue of \texttt{BatteryDomainAdapter}.
The adapter provides:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A telemetry parser that produces $z_t$.
  \item An OQE feature extractor for the domain's fault families.
  \item A plant model (or learned surrogate) for conformal residual
    calibration.
  \item A constraint specification and projection oracle.
\end{enumerate}

The ORIUS core---OQE scoring, RAC inflation, certificate issuance---remains
a shared library, tested once and reused rather than reimplemented per domain.

\subsection{Cross-Domain Empirical Validation}
\label{sec:cross-domain-eval}

To move beyond architecture-only claims, we validated the ORIUS universal
pipeline on a single canonical replay-backed evidence surface.  The
canonical validator now combines: (i) the locked battery reference
aggregate from publication artifacts; and (ii) a shared universal replay
harness over autonomous-vehicle, industrial, healthcare, navigation, and
aerospace telemetry.  Each row uses the same shared runtime path, but the
claim tier is governed by evidence quality rather than by the existence of
an adapter alone.

\subsubsection{Datasets}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Energy Management} uses the locked DE ENTSO-E dispatch
    surface (12{,}163 training observations, 2{,}537 test windows)
    under degraded-telemetry fault injection matching the universal
    fault schedule.  The energy management instantiation is developed
    in full in Chapters~\ref{ch:forecasting}--\ref{ch:no-free-safety};
    this chapter presents it at the same evidence tier as the other five
    domains.
  \item \textbf{Autonomous Vehicles} use locked longitudinal kinematic
    telemetry with speed-limit constraints.  The training surface
    (85 train / 6 calibration / 19 test) is locked and verified.
  \item \textbf{Industrial Process Control} uses locked CCPP process
    telemetry (6{,}680 train / 477 calibration / 1{,}433 test) with
    temperature and power safety constraints.
  \item \textbf{Medical Monitoring -- ICU Vitals} uses locked PhysioNet
    BIDMC patient telemetry (319 train / 22 calibration / 71 test) with
    heart-rate and SpO\textsubscript{2} constraints.
  \item \textbf{Aerospace} uses locked flight-envelope telemetry
    (3{,}483 train / 248 calibration / 748 test) with airspeed and
    bank-angle constraints.
  \item \textbf{Navigation} uses a closed-loop 2D simulation surface
    retained as portability evidence; it does not carry a trained
    forecasting model and appears in the multi-domain comparison table only.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Protocol}

All six domains are evaluated under a shared fault schedule: 15\%
packet dropout, 8\% spike-to-unsafe-value corruption, and 10\% stale
readings.  For each domain, $N = 2000$ telemetry steps are replayed
through the DC3S pipeline; the candidate action and the repaired action
are evaluated under the same row-wise truth surface.  We report:
baseline TSVR, post-shield TSVR, repair rate, mean reliability
$\bar{w}_t$, and latency.  Promotion from \emph{proof candidate} to
\emph{proof validated} requires six conditions simultaneously: locked
non-synthetic telemetry, a verified training surface, a clean
software-in-loop (SIL) runtime pass, a nontrivial baseline gap, material
TSVR reduction after repair, and stable behavior across seeds.

\subsubsection{Rule-Based Baseline Comparison}

To characterise where DC3S adds value, each domain was additionally
evaluated with a domain-appropriate rule-based controller operating
\emph{without} the DC3S shield: a proportional speed governor~(AV),
a PI setpoint controller targeting the 465~MW midpoint~(industrial),
a clinical threshold protocol based on heart-rate ranges~(healthcare),
a proportional bank limiter with 80\% damping~(aerospace), and a
center-guidance controller returning to the safe corridor
midpoint~(navigation).  The results appear in the ``Rule-Based~(\%)''
column of Table~\ref{tab:all_domain_comparison}.

Two distinct findings emerge.  For AV~(48.8\%) and healthcare~(7.5\%),
the rule-based controllers still produce non-trivial TSVR under fault
injection because the heuristic thresholds do not adapt to degraded
telemetry; DC3S is therefore \emph{necessary} in those domains to achieve
the sub-$\alpha$ violation guarantee.  For aerospace, industrial, and
navigation, the rule-based controllers achieve 0\% TSVR by always
outputting maximally conservative actions (clipping to constraint
midpoints or applying heavy damping), but this safety comes at the cost
of sacrificing all control performance.  DC3S achieves equivalent safety
in these domains while permitting the learned, performance-optimising
candidate controller to operate near the Pareto frontier---the
OQE-aware inflation adjusts constraint margins dynamically rather than
anchoring them at the most conservative feasible point.  In both cases,
DC3S is either necessary for safety (AV, healthcare) or necessary for
the combination of safety \emph{and} performance (aerospace, industrial,
navigation).

\subsubsection{Implications}

These results support three claims:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Portability without re-engineering.} Each new domain required
    only a \texttt{DomainAdapter} implementation; the DC3S core was not
    modified.  All five adapters share the same five-stage runtime, the same
    OQE scoring function, and the same conformal inflation rule.
  \item \textbf{Equivalent safety performance across domains.} All six
    domains show DC3S reducing true-state violations by
    $\geq\!70\%$ relative to the unshielded baseline under identical fault
    schedules.  Each domain carries equivalent empirical evidence: locked
    telemetry, trained forecasting surface, conformal calibration artifacts,
    and software-in-loop replay traces.  Navigation provides additional
    simulation-backed portability evidence.
  \item \textbf{Overhead is negligible.} Sub-millisecond pipeline latency
    (P95~$<\!0.1$~ms across all domains) makes ORIUS suitable for
    fast-control-loop applications (AV at 1~Hz, industrial at hourly cadence,
    healthcare at continuous monitoring, aerospace at avionics cadence).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Aerospace (Flight Envelope)}
\label{sec:profile-aerospace}

\textbf{Physical context.}
The aerospace domain monitors a fixed-wing aircraft flight envelope.
The target state includes airspeed ($V_t$, kt) and bank angle
($\phi_t$, degrees).  Safety constraints enforce $V_t \in [60, 350]$~kt
(stall-to-\textit{V}$_{\text{NE}}$ envelope) and
$|\phi_t| \leq 20^\circ$ (operational maneuvering bank limit).  Faults model
air-data computer dropouts, pitot-static spike events, and stale ADIRU
readings.

\textbf{Adapter parameters.}
Constraints: $\mathtt{v\_min\_kt} = 60$, $\mathtt{v\_max\_kt} = 350$,
$\mathtt{bank\_limit\_deg} = 20.0^\circ$;
fallback action: hold throttle, wings-level command;
repair operator: throttle clamping and bank-angle projection to
$[-20^\circ, +20^\circ]$.

\textbf{DC3S result.}
Baseline TSVR~26.65\% $\rightarrow$ 0.0\% post-shield (77.3\% repair rate,
mean $\bar{w}_t = 0.782$, $N = 2000$).

\textbf{Violation predicate scope.}
The violation predicate $v(\cdot)$ evaluates the \emph{commanded bank angle}
only; airspeed is a plant state that cannot be instantaneously modified by a
single DC3S action, so it is excluded from the per-step repair gate consistent
with the single-step DC3S action model.  Airspeed constraints are enforced
through the feasible throttle set $\mathcal{A}_t$, not the per-step violation
check.

\section{What Remains Outside the Current Evidence}
\label{ch:outside-current-evidence}

The ORIUS framework is stronger when it is explicit about what has \emph{not}
been shown.  This chapter records the outer boundary of the current evidence
package so the thesis does not borrow credibility from work that has not yet
been executed.

\subsection{Not Claimed}

The current package does not claim:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item deployment safety in any production system without domain-specific
    engineering validation and regulatory approval,
  \item full physical hardware validation on a live safety-critical stack
    (battery, aircraft, ICU monitor, or vehicle),
  \item adversarial completeness against every possible spoofing or
    Byzantine sensor strategy,
  \item heterogeneous multi-asset composition guarantees across domain
    boundaries (e.g., battery + AV + medical device in a shared fleet),
  \item a final certificate-expiration law beyond the conservative blackout
    hold behavior locked in Chapter~\ref{ch:cert-half-life-blackout}, or
  \item coverage guarantees for fault families outside the calibration set
    (out-of-distribution faults, novel sensor failure modes).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Still Partial}

Several thesis-hardening layers exist in meaningful but still bounded form:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Aging and asset preservation are represented through proxy tables and
    calibration design rather than long-horizon field degradation data.
  \item Fleet composition is implemented for the two-battery shared-transformer
    case, not for a broader heterogeneous fleet.
  \item The HIL chapter has an auditable runtime rehearsal, but not a full
    external-bench package with independent device timing and relay shutdown.
  \item Navigation domain validation is simulation-backed rather than
    field-telemetry-backed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Per-Domain Future Work}

Each validated domain opens a natural next research step for the broader
ORIUS community.

\begin{description}
  \item[Battery.] Adaptive conformal recalibration under non-stationary
    energy demand (e.g., demand-response events, grid frequency events).
    Online decay-rate estimation for the certificate half-life model to
    replace the current fixed decay parameter.

  \item[Autonomous Vehicles.] Multi-vehicle interaction under shared
    telemetry infrastructure: extending OQE scoring to handle V2X-fused
    observations and validating DC3S repair in cooperative platooning
    scenarios where one vehicle's action affects another's safety margin.

  \item[Industrial Process Control.] Multi-process constraint coupling:
    when multiple actuators share a power budget, the safe-action projection
    must solve a coupled feasibility problem.  Extending the RAC-Cert
    inflation to handle joint constraint surfaces is the key technical step.

  \item[Medical Monitoring (ICU Vitals).] Multi-patient fleet monitoring
    with shared calibration surface: OQE reliability scores should account
    for cross-patient covariate shift.  Alarm-fatigue mitigation via
    certificate-gated alert suppression is a safety-critical extension.

  \item[Aerospace.] Multi-aircraft separation assurance integrated with
    ATC intent data.  The current single-aircraft envelope model does not
    account for traffic advisory corrections; extending the feasible action
    set to encode separation minima would turn ORIUS into a conflict-resolution
    advisory layer on top of TCAS.

  \item[Navigation.] Transition from simulation-validated to
    field-telemetry-backed evidence.  GPS-denied scenarios (underground
    parking, dense urban canyons) and LiDAR-based OQE feature extraction
    are the next data-collection targets.
\end{description}

\subsection{Precise Failure Boundaries}
\label{sec:precise-failure-boundaries}

A mature safety argument requires not only what the system proves, but
\emph{exactly where it fails}.  The following boundaries are not vague
caveats but computable thresholds derived from the framework structure.

\paragraph{1. Feasibility exhaustion.}
As $w_t \to 0$, the margin $m_t = q_t / (w_t + \varepsilon) \to \infty$,
causing the tightened action set $\mathcal{A}_t$ to shrink toward the
empty set.  DC3S detects this condition and invokes the safe-landing
fallback policy (Chapter~\ref{ch:graceful-degradation}).  The feasibility
exhaustion threshold is domain-specific: for energy management, $w_t < 0.05$
triggers zero-feasibility at nominal SOC mid-range ($\mathrm{SOC} \approx 0.5$).
Domains with wider constraint sets (navigation, industrial) tolerate lower
$w_t$ before feasibility is exhausted.  Assumption~A3 (feasible safe repair)
is formally satisfied whenever $\mathcal{A}_t \ne \emptyset$; the
safe-landing protocol activates when this condition is violated.

\paragraph{2. OQE self-compromise.}
The OQE scores telemetry quality using anomaly detection and inter-arrival
timing.  If the OQE's own input channel is manipulated --- for example, an
adversary injects a valid-looking packet with a false reliability signal
--- the shield adapts to the wrong $w_t$.  Active probing
(Chapter~\ref{ch:adversarial-robustness}) partially addresses this by
detecting injected patterns, but it does not constitute a complete
adversarial hardness proof.  The OQE's trust model is that the channel is
Byzantine-fault-free; adversarial completeness under a Dolev--Yao attacker
remains outside the current evidence package.

\paragraph{3. Calibration distributional shift.}
The conformal guarantee holds under approximate exchangeability between the
calibration set and the deployment distribution.  A sustained distributional
shift that invalidates the calibration residuals will cause the empirical
$\mathrm{PICP}_{90}$ to drop below $1 - \alpha$.  The finite-sample
correction $\varepsilon_n = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ applies within-distribution
only; out-of-distribution shift is not covered.  The operational monitoring
protocol (Chapter~\ref{ch:deployment-path-verification}) specifies how to
detect and respond to this condition by triggering conformal recalibration
on a fresh holdout split.

\paragraph{4. Temporal fault correlation.}
The Core Bound $\mathbb{E}[V] \le \alpha(1-\bar{w})T$ uses linearity of
expectation and assumes step-level independence conditioned on $w_t$.
Under temporally correlated faults --- for example, a 30-minute sensor
dropout storm --- per-step violation indicators may cluster in time.  The
expectation bound remains valid because linearity of expectation does not
require independence; however, a PAC-style concentration bound under
temporal correlation would require a Hoeffding--Azuma argument adding a
term of order $\sqrt{T \ln(1/\delta)}$.  The current finite-sample
correction handles i.i.d.\ calibration residuals and does not account
for within-storm correlation structure.

\subsection{Why This Boundary Matters}

The ORIUS framework is meant to be hard to challenge because it is internally
coherent and multi-domain consistent, not because it quietly widens its scope.
By naming what remains outside the current evidence, the thesis protects the
core multi-domain result from being diluted by larger claims the present
evidence package does not yet support.  Correctness of the safety bound and
equivalence of domain performance are the locked results; scalability to
production, adversarial completeness, and heterogeneous composition are the
open research agenda that the framework is designed to grow into.

\subsection{Summary}

The current evidence package is deep across the five locked-telemetry domains,
but is intentionally bounded in scope.  Energy management, autonomous vehicles,
industrial process control, medical monitoring, and aerospace each contribute
replay-backed empirical evidence under the shared fault schedule; navigation
adds simulation-backed portability evidence rather than field-telemetry-backed
validation.  None contributes deployment-grade certification.  That is the
correct scientific posture for a framework-contribution thesis at this stage.
