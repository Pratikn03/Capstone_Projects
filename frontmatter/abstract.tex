\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This dissertation develops a battery-first version of ORIUS (Observation--Reality Integrity
for Battery Safety), a framework for safe action under degraded observation in physical
AI systems.  The central claim is that a controller can appear safe on observed state while
being physically unsafe on true state when the observation channel degrades.  In the battery
domain, this hidden failure takes the form of dispatch decisions that appear feasible under
observed state of charge while violating the true battery envelope.

The dissertation uses grid-scale battery dispatch as the first complete validation surface
because the state, constraints, and telemetry pathways are interpretable, physically meaningful,
and experimentally reproducible.  The battery-domain architecture centres on \dcs{}, a
runtime layer placed at the optimise-to-dispatch boundary.  \dcs{} scores telemetry quality,
inflates uncertainty when reliability weakens, constrains the safe action set, repairs unsafe
candidate actions, and emits auditable runtime certificates.  The evaluation framework,
CPSBench, maintains separate truth and observed trajectories so that hidden safety failure
becomes measurable rather than implicit.

Under degraded telemetry, a quality-ignorant deterministic controller produces a true-state
violation rate (TSVR) of 3.9\% with P95 severity of 333.4~MWh, while appearing perfectly
safe in observed state.  DC3S eliminates all true-state violations (TSVR = 0\%) with
an intervention rate of only 2.8\%, demonstrating that the observation--action safety gap
(OASG) is both real and closable.

Beyond the core empirical result, the dissertation establishes a four-theorem ladder
(OASG existence, one-step safety preservation, the ORIUS core safety bound, and no-free-safety
necessity), extends the battery validation to fault-performance stress tests, latency
benchmarking, conditional coverage audits, hyperparameter stability analysis,
hardware-in-the-loop trials, certificate half-life studies, graceful degradation protocols,
battery-fleet compositional safety, and adversarial robustness via active probing.

The battery domain is the only locked theorem-to-artifact validation surface in this
dissertation.  Four additional non-battery domains---autonomous vehicles,
industrial process control, medical monitoring, and aerospace---provide
equivalent replay-depth empirical validation under the shared universal
harness.  Navigation remains simulation-backed portability evidence rather
than part of the defended five-domain claim.
