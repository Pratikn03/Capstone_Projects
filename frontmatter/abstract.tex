\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This dissertation presents ORIUS (Observation--Reality Integrity for Universal Safety), a
framework for safe action under degraded observation in cyber-physical systems (CPS).
The central claim is that a controller can appear safe on observed state while being
physically unsafe on true state when the observation channel degrades.  This
observation--action safety gap (OASG) is latent in any CPS that separates sensing from
actuation, and ORIUS provides a domain-agnostic architecture for detecting, bounding, and
eliminating it.

The framework is evaluated across six peer CPS domains: energy management (grid-scale
battery dispatch), autonomous vehicles, industrial process control, medical monitoring,
aerospace control, and navigation.  All six share the same five-stage DC3S pipeline---Detect,
Calibrate, Constrain, Shield, Certify---instantiated through a common \texttt{DomainAdapter}
interface.  The evaluation harness, CPSBench, maintains separate truth and observed
trajectories across all domains so that hidden safety failure becomes measurable
rather than implicit.

The energy management domain provides the formally locked theorem-to-artifact surface.
Under degraded telemetry, a quality-ignorant deterministic controller produces a true-state
violation rate (TSVR) of 3.9\% with P95 severity of 333.4~MWh, while appearing perfectly
safe in observed state.  DC3S eliminates all true-state violations (TSVR~$= 0$\%) for the energy management
domain, with an intervention rate of only 2.8\%, demonstrating that the OASG is both
real and closable.  TSVR reductions to zero are confirmed under the locked three-seed
evaluation protocol across the industrial process control and medical monitoring domains
(proof-validated).  The autonomous vehicle and aerospace domains do not achieve
TSVR~$= 0$\% under the locked protocol---AV remains at 2.78\% baseline (constraint
structure mismatch; classified proof-candidate) and aerospace at 9.72\% (experimental
surface)---and are distinguished from the proof-validated tier accordingly.
The navigation domain provides simulation-backed portability evidence (0.69\% TSVR).

The dissertation establishes a four-theorem safety ladder: (T1) OASG existence, (T2)
one-step safety preservation, (T3) the ORIUS core safety bound
$\mathbb{E}[V] \le \alpha(1-\bar{w})T$, and (T4) no-free-safety necessity.  The
finite-sample correction $\varepsilon_n = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ tightens the bound for
calibration sets of finite size.  These theorems are artifact-backed: each is linked
to a locked experimental trace that confirms the predicted behaviour with measurable
empirical slack.

The dissertation further characterises DC3S through fault-performance stress tests,
latency benchmarking, conditional coverage audits, hyperparameter stability analysis,
software-in-the-loop trials, certificate half-life studies, graceful degradation protocols,
compositional safety for multi-unit fleets, and adversarial robustness via active probing.
Across the three proof-validated domains and under all tested fault conditions,
DC3S achieves the core safety guarantee while preserving dispatch performance.
The framework demonstrates that the observation--action safety gap is a universal
CPS phenomenon with a principled remedy---closable on the reference and
proof-validated rows, partially resolved on proof-candidate surfaces, and
structurally addressed on experimental surfaces.
